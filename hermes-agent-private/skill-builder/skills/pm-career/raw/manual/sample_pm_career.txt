## On the Promotion vs. Switch Decision

The decision to stay and push for promotion vs. switching companies is one of the most commonly mishandled career decisions PMs make. Most people frame it wrong: they think about title and compensation when they should be thinking about learning rate and decision authority.

The first question isn't "what will they give me?" It's "am I actually hitting a skill ceiling, or is this an organizational ceiling?" These feel identical from the inside but require completely different responses.

Signs you're in a skill gap (stay and fix it):
- You've been passed over but different managers have given consistent feedback about the same 2–3 things
- When you watch a more senior PM operate, you can identify specific behaviors you don't yet have
- You're being asked to do things you can't confidently execute yet
- Your proposals get rejected for reasons you find defensible, not arbitrary

Signs you're in an org ceiling (probably leave):
- Your performance reviews are strong but promotions go to people with political capital you don't have
- You've solved the problems you were hired to solve and there's no new scope available
- The PM role at your level hasn't changed in 2+ years
- Your manager is not in a position to advocate for you — they're political, checked out, or below you in influence
- Decisions that should be yours are getting pulled upward or sideways

The dangerous middle: you might be in both. A PM who hasn't grown AND works in a political environment. In this case, leaving is probably right, but you need to be honest about what you're carrying into your next role.

## On the Value of Scope vs. Title

The single most common mistake mid-career PMs make: optimizing for title when they should be optimizing for scope.

Title without scope is a career trap. You become a Senior PM at a company where Senior PM means "manages a single small feature with one eng." The title looks good on LinkedIn. The work doesn't build the skills that make you genuinely senior.

Scope means: the blast radius of your decisions, the number of stakeholders whose work depends on your judgment, the strategic ambiguity you're expected to resolve, the organizational surface area you're accountable to.

A PM II at a high-growth company who owns a critical infrastructure strategy is getting more development than a Senior PM at a 500-person company who owns a checkout flow that three engineers touch.

The question to ask before taking any role: "In this role, what's the most important decision I'll make in the next 12 months?" If the answer isn't immediately interesting to you, that's a signal.

Second question: "Can my manager clearly articulate what the next level looks like, and give me two examples of people who got there?" If not, there's no defined path.

## On Switching Companies

When switching companies actually makes sense:
1. You've exhausted the internal growth path — not just "my manager said not this cycle," but genuinely no new scope is available
2. The company's trajectory is misaligned with where the industry is going
3. You're not getting exposure to decision-making at the level you need for the next stage
4. Your network inside the company is not growing your external credibility

When it's the wrong reason to switch:
- Chasing a hot company for the brand — often means becoming a cog, losing decision authority, slower feedback loops
- Avoiding hard feedback by finding a place that will take you at a higher title
- The grass-is-greener fallacy — your problems frequently travel with you
- Switching before you've absorbed the lessons of your current environment

The interview meta-point: the interview process is designed to evaluate whether you can do the job. But you should be using the process to evaluate whether the job will make you better. Ask about the last two big decisions the team made and why. If you can't get a clear answer, walk away.

## What Senior Actually Means

In the PM world, "senior" has two definitions that rarely align:

1. The HR definition: tenure + competency signals that get you the title band
2. The market definition: someone who can own a problem from ambiguous to shipped with minimal oversight

Most companies promote to the HR definition. The market rewards the second.

Senior PM thinking:
- Starting from the user and the market, not the roadmap
- Making the space argument, not just the feature argument: "we shouldn't build any of these — here's the real problem"
- Knowing when to kill a project vs. save it
- Building stakeholder capital proactively, not reactively
- Treating eng and design as strategic partners whose judgment changes your judgment
- Holding ambiguity without resolving it prematurely

Junior PM thinking:
- Treating the roadmap as immutable
- Seeking permission rather than building conviction
- Defining success as "shipped" rather than "worked"
- Reacting to escalations rather than preventing them
- Confusing activity with impact
- Framing every problem as a feature request

## On the MBA Question

The MBA question is almost always a proxy for a different question. Usually: "Am I advancing fast enough, and if not, why not?"

MBA makes sense for PM careers in specific cases:
- You want to pivot from a non-product background and need the network + legitimacy signal
- You want to move into a domain where the MBA is genuinely a filter (consulting, PE-backed product, certain enterprise sales-led companies)
- You want to make a geographic or sector jump and the cohort is the fastest path

MBA is probably the wrong move if:
- You're already in a PM role and progressing, even if slowly
- Your goal is to get to Group PM or Director at a tech company — the MBA doesn't accelerate this, internal advocacy and track record do
- You're doing it because you don't know what else to do

The real cost of an MBA isn't tuition. It's 2 years of not compounding your execution track record. A PM who spends 2 years at a great company learning the craft comes out with more leverage than an MBA who needs to rebuild credibility from scratch.

## On Reading Organizational Signals

The best PMs read their organization the way a navigator reads a ship — they know where power flows, where decisions actually get made, and whose opinion shapes outcomes before anything is written down.

Signals worth tracking:
- Who gets invited to the room when strategy is being set?
- Who do senior leaders ask when they need a quick read on something?
- Who gets resources when there's a fight over headcount?
- Whose roadmap concerns make it into the quarterly plan without a battle?

If you're not in any of those conversations after 18 months, ask why. Either the organization doesn't have room for you, or you haven't built the credibility to be included. Both are diagnostic, but they require different responses.

The failure mode: mistaking exclusion from those rooms as proof of an org ceiling when it's actually a signal that your stakeholder relationships are thinner than you think.

## Staying vs. Leaving — The Real Calculus

The question isn't "should I stay or should I go." It's: "In 3 years, will I be a better PM if I stay here vs. somewhere else — and what's my confidence in that assessment?"

Most people overweight the present (compensation, comfort, known environment) and underweight the future (learning rate, market positioning, skill trajectory).

The fastest way to calibrate: find two people who are 5 years ahead of you in career trajectory. One who stayed, one who left. Ask them what they would change. The pattern of their regrets is more informative than any salary benchmark.

A useful diagnostic: write down the three hardest problems you worked on in the last 12 months. If you struggled to list three — or if the hardest problems weren't actually hard — that's a more honest signal than your performance review score.

## On Company Stage and Career Stage

Early-career PMs often benefit most from larger, more process-heavy companies where they can learn craft without being exposed too early to ambiguity they're not ready for.

Mid-career PMs typically need the opposite: more ambiguity, more ownership, more exposure to decisions that have real stakes and real consequences.

The mistake: mid-career PMs who stay in a large, structured environment past the point where it's teaching them anything. The structure becomes a comfort, not a scaffold.

Early-stage startups teach certain things extremely well: ownership, scrappiness, prioritization under extreme constraints, stakeholder compression (fewer people to align). They teach other things poorly: craft, process, scaling, managing up in complex orgs.

Neither is better. The question is what you need at this stage of your development.

## On Ownership as a Career Signal

"Ownership" is one of the most overloaded words in PM job descriptions. Here's how to tell if you'd actually have it:

1. Ask what the last two significant decisions were that the PM in this role made independently, without needing approval
2. Ask what happened when the PM got something wrong — who owned the consequence?
3. Ask what's on the roadmap that the PM pushed back on and won

If they can't answer these specifically, the ownership is nominal, not real. Nominal ownership is worse than no ownership because it teaches you to feel responsible without the authority to act.

Real ownership means you can say no. It means your judgment about priority is respected even when it conflicts with what a stakeholder wants. It means when you're wrong, you feel it.

## Diagnostic Framework for Career Decision Situations

When helping someone with a PM career decision, these are the questions that distinguish cases that look the same on the surface:

1. "In the last 6 months, did your scope of decisions expand or contract?" — If it contracted without explanation, that's an org signal, not a skill signal.

2. "Can you name 3 decisions you made in the last month that your manager doesn't know about?" — If you can't, your ownership is thinner than your title suggests.

3. "What specific feedback has been consistent across more than one manager or review?" — Consistent multi-source feedback is usually a skill gap. Inconsistent or politically-colored feedback is usually an org signal.

4. "If you left tomorrow, what would be missing that you haven't learned yet?" — If the answer is "nothing significant," it's time to leave regardless of comp.

5. "Does your current manager understand what you want to do next, and can they describe a path to it?" — If not, they can't advocate for you effectively even if they want to.

6. "In your current role, when did you last surprise yourself — do something you didn't know you could do?" — If you can't remember, the role isn't growing you.
