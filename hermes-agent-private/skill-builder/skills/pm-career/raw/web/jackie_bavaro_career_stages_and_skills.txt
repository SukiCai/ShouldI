## The 3 Stages of a Product Manager's Career

"The entire definition of being 'great' changes at each stage."

### Stage 1: Shipping Products (Intern → PM 2)

Focus: Executing the core product lifecycle — Discover, Define, Design, Develop, Deliver, Debrief.

Success = delivering products that delight customers and hit business goals.

PMs improve through better product sense, analytical abilities, and execution. But mastery of these skills alone won't lead to promotion beyond PM 2. You can be an excellent executor and hit a ceiling.

### Stage 2: Product Strategy (Senior PM, PM Lead)

The job fundamentally changes. Rather than receiving a defined scope and executing it, you must now:
- Identify new opportunities yourself
- Put together a plan for how to win them
- Set goals, not just hit them

"Excellence involves developing winning strategies that help their product succeed in the marketplace."

The distinction that matters: Stage 1 PMs execute against a defined area. Stage 2 PMs define the area and make the case for why it's the right area.

### Stage 3: Organizational Excellence (Director, Senior Director)

The job transforms again:
- Build and manage a great team
- Hire, coach, establish processes for organizational output beyond what you could do as a single PM
- Serve as a strategic advisor to the rest of the company
- Attend and contribute at executive-level conversations

## Two Types of Advancement (and Why They're Different)

**Within-stage promotion**: Craft improvement — executing current responsibilities faster and better. Visible and relatively straightforward.

**Cross-stage promotion**: Requires intentionality. Often feels like stepping backward initially, because new responsibilities pull attention from the current role's execution. You will appear less competent for a period before becoming more competent at the new level.

The mistake most PMs make: treating a cross-stage transition like a within-stage promotion. They try to get promoted by being better at Stage 1 work, but Stage 2 requires doing fundamentally different work.

## Surprising Skills That Matter at Senior Levels

### 1. Building Trust Deliberately

Senior PMs must demonstrate sound judgment — but judgment is invisible unless you show your work.

Tactics:
- Unpack your decision-making frameworks explicitly: "Here's how I thought about this, here are the alternatives I considered, here's why I chose this path"
- Avoid surprises: "Do you proactively share the right information up and down?"
- Maintain reliable execution through checklists and proactive communication (if you say you'll do something, do it or flag it early)

### 2. Understanding Organizational Dynamics

"Learn how work actually gets done beyond official processes."

This means:
- Identifying key stakeholders and what they actually care about (often different from what they say they care about)
- Understanding gatekeepers' preferences and what triggers resistance
- Recognizing when scrappiness and rule-bending serve the broader mission better than strict process adherence

### 3. Strategic Alignment

Connect your work to company strategy explicitly. Don't assume the connection is obvious to executives — articulate it.

"Articulate an inspiring vision" that energizes teams and builds leader confidence in your direction. A senior PM who can't explain why their area matters at the company level will be overlooked in resource allocation.

### 4. Collaboration & Relationships

Develop relationships across the company so that leaders actively want to collaborate with you during escalations — not just tolerate you.

Assessment: 360-degree feedback. Are you viewed as constructive, humble, and genuinely collaborative? Or defensive and self-interested? Executives pattern-match on this heavily when deciding who gets expanded scope.

### 5. Recruiting Capability

At senior levels, you will be asked to help recruit. Senior PMs who can't write compelling job descriptions, pitch candidates, or conduct effective conversations limit their team's growth — and their own.

## Key Framework: Scope, Autonomy, Impact

"Career ladders mislead people. Promotions depend on scope, autonomy, and impact — delivering impactful work at higher levels, not mastering every listed skill on a competency rubric."

This means:
- You can be technically competent on every listed competency and still not get promoted if you're operating at the wrong scope
- The path to promotion is: expand scope first, demonstrate impact at that scope, then ask for the title

## On Strategic Thinking

Many people get told they need to "be more strategic" and have no idea what that means.

Strategic thinking for PMs specifically means:
- Starting from goals rather than solutions (the opposite of feature-think)
- Validating ideas early with real people before committing resources
- The greatest product thinkers don't get ideas right on the first try — they test and iterate based on feedback

"Reducing development time from six months to two weeks" is possible if you validate before building rather than building before validating.
