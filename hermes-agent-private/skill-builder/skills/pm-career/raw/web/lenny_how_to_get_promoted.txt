## Personal Context

Lenny got promoted from a mid-level PM (L5) to Senior PM (L6) after leading a mission-critical project (Instant Book) for several years. The project spanned multiple departments, reported up to the executive staff, and became a major part of company strategy. The key ingredient he identifies in retrospect: having someone with real influence fight for him in the calibration process.

---

## The Seven Strategies

### 1. Deliver more impact
Focus on business growth, not just shipping features. Lead important projects with real stakes. Highlight wins clearly — don't assume your manager sees everything you do. Make the impact legible.

### 2. Take on more scope
Demonstrate readiness for the next level by handling increased responsibility before the promotion. Don't wait to be asked — identify scope you can credibly own and start owning it.

### 3. Address your gaps
Identify 2–3 specific limiting skills and create an action plan with your manager. The key move: make your growth visible. Monthly tracking of progress on named gaps, reviewed together with your manager, keeps the conversation grounded and makes it harder to be passed over without explanation.

### 4. Find an influential champion
"The most underappreciated, and often the most powerful, ingredient in getting promoted is to have someone with influence fight for you in the calibration process."

Promotion decisions happen in rooms you're not in. Your direct manager may advocate for you, but if they lack organizational influence, the advocacy doesn't move the needle. Identify who in the org has influence at the calibration level — and build a real relationship with them.

### 5. Pay attention to who gets promoted
Study promotion patterns in your organization. Who gets promoted? What did they work on? What's the narrative about them? Pattern-match to understand what the organization actually rewards vs. what it says it rewards. These are often different.

### 6. Ask for it directly
Have explicit conversations about promotion timing. Many PMs assume their manager is tracking this — often they're not, or they're not prioritizing it without a direct signal of urgency from you. Asking directly is not aggressive; it's information.

### 7. Quit and go elsewhere
Sometimes external moves are the only realistic path. If you've been passed over multiple cycles with the same feedback, or if the organization simply doesn't have headroom at the next level, staying becomes a net negative on your career trajectory. The market often gives you the next-level title faster than your current employer will.

## Important Caveats on Promotions

Practical limits that exist regardless of performance:
- Minimum tenure requirements per level
- Role availability at the next level (headcount may be frozen)
- Budget constraints in a given review cycle
- Readiness assessment relative to peers, not just absolute standards

Understanding these limits is itself a senior PM skill. If you're blocked by structural constraints rather than performance gaps, you need to know that — and the action is different.

---

## Framework: The Monthly Career Coaching Action Plan

Track 2–3 specific skill gaps in a shared doc with your manager. For each gap:
- Name it precisely
- Agree on what "fixed" looks like
- Track status monthly
- Update together in 1:1s

This keeps promotion conversations grounded in evidence and removes ambiguity about what's needed.
