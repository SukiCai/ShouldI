## Core Framework: Treat Yourself as a Product

Biddle's central idea: your career is a product. That means you should apply the same thinking to it that great PMs apply to their products:
- Form hypotheses about what will work
- Run experiments to test them (side projects, informational interviews, strategic moves)
- Build feedback systems to evaluate progress
- Iterate based on what you learn

This reframe changes the emotional relationship to career decisions. Rather than anxious hope, you're running experiments. Failure of an experiment is information, not verdict.

## The Three Pillars

### 1. Do the Job (Required Skills)

Technical skills: technology understanding, management, creativity, business acumen, marketing, design, consumer science.

Functional leadership skills: leadership, team management, strategic thinking, results orientation, culture-building, business maturity, domain expertise.

The honest observation: the skills listed in job descriptions don't predict career success as much as people think. What matters is your ability to learn new skills quickly as the job changes — meta-learning, not static competency.

### 2. Embrace Creativity and Risk (Career Hacking)

Key metric: "Make it an 8." Biddle targets an 8/10 job satisfaction as the navigation metric for career decisions. Not 10/10 — that's not realistic and leads to paralysis. Not 5/10 — that's enduring rather than building. 8/10 means you're engaged, growing, and pulling others upward.

Testing career hypotheses:
- Take on side projects that expose you to domains you're curious about before making a full move
- Do informational interviews at companies you're considering — not to get a job, but to gather data
- Volunteer for cross-functional work that builds new relationships and skills
- Accept learning curves as the cost of growth rather than signs of incompetence

### 3. Extend Your Career (Personal Board of Directors)

Build a small, diverse group of people who can give you honest feedback and different perspectives:
- Mentors in product (people ahead of you in the trajectory you want)
- Peers at similar career stages (reference class for "is this normal?")
- Mentors outside product (finance, engineering, design, ops — different lenses on the same problems)
- People who will tell you hard truths, not just encourage you

The distinction: a sponsor advocates for you in rooms you're not in. A mentor advises you directly. You need both.

## Career Progression Model

Biddle outlines career stages not as titles but as the scale of the thing you're building:

1. **Build a feature** — APM / PM
2. **Build a product** — PM / Senior PM
3. **Build a business** — Group PM / Director
4. **Build an organization** — VP Product
5. **Build an industry** — CPO / founder

The transition between stages is not incremental. It requires giving up things you were good at. The VP who tries to keep being a great Senior PM will fail at being a VP. Knowing when to let go of what made you successful is a senior leadership competency.

## Practical Advice from Biddle

**On seeking mentors:**
- Make specific, concrete asks — "Can we talk for 30 minutes about how you decided to move from company X to company Y?" is answerable. "Can you be my mentor?" is not.
- The persistent, specific outreach gets responses. Generic asks get ignored.

**On learning curves:**
- Stay comfortable with learning curves. If you're always competent, you're not growing.
- The periods when you feel most incompetent are often the periods of highest learning.

**On job satisfaction as navigation:**
- Regularly rate your job satisfaction on a 1–10 scale and track it over time. If it's been below 7 for six months, something needs to change. If it's been below 5 for three months, the change is urgent.

**On relationships:**
- Build relationships during good times. The people you stayed in touch with when you didn't need anything are the ones who will go out of their way to help you later.

## On Large vs. Small Companies

Large companies teach:
- How to work within structured processes
- How to navigate organizational complexity
- How to influence without direct authority
- How to build and maintain alignment across many stakeholders

Small companies teach:
- How to operate with radical ownership
- How to prioritize under extreme resource constraints
- How to move faster than the problem space changes
- How to see the whole business, not just your slice

Neither is categorically better. The question is what you're missing and what your next stage of growth needs. The mid-career PM who has only been at large companies often lacks the ownership instinct. The mid-career PM who has only been at startups often lacks the ability to operate at scale.
