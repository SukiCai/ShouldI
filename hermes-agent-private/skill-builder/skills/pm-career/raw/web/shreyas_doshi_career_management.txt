## On Communication and How You're Judged

"Presentations make up five percent of your job, but fifty percent of how you're judged."

This is one of the most counterintuitive and practically important realities of senior PM careers. The work happens in product decisions, user research, roadmap calls. But how you're perceived — and therefore whether you get promoted, given more scope, or advocated for — is disproportionately determined by how you communicate in high-visibility moments.

Implication: investing in communication skills (writing, speaking, presenting) has an outsized career return relative to the percentage of the job it represents.

## Frameworks: Title–Money–Scope and Tablestakes–Ego–Joy

These are two frameworks Shreyas uses for PM career decisions.

**Title–Money–Scope**: When evaluating a role or promotion, separate these three dimensions. Most people unconsciously weight title and money heavily because they're legible. Scope (the actual decision authority and surface area of your work) is often underweighted but is the most important predictor of long-term career development.

**Tablestakes–Ego–Joy**: When assessing current or future work:
- Tablestakes: the basic requirements of the role (non-negotiable)
- Ego: what makes you look good externally (often overweighted)
- Joy: what you actually find meaningful and energizing (often underweighted)

Most bad career decisions involve overweighting Ego and underweighting Joy. A role that sounds impressive but you find draining accelerates career stagnation, not growth.

## On Managing Upward for Promotion

Three approaches to promotion:
1. **Hope** your manager recognizes your brilliance (passive — low yield)
2. **Demand** promotion directly (adversarial — damages relationships)
3. **Partner** with your manager by asking: "What gap exists between where I am now and the next level?"

The partnership approach transforms your manager into an ally rather than an adversary. It makes the conversation collaborative and gives your manager a clear role in your growth.

"Career growth ultimately rests on you, not your manager." But your manager can be a powerful accelerant or a ceiling — knowing which one you have is critical information.

## On Promotion Committees and Bias

Promotion decisions often suffer from presentation bias. Decision-makers in calibration committees may judge candidates based on one high-visibility presentation rather than sustained impact. This systematically disadvantages:
- Non-native English speakers
- Introverts and quiet contributors
- People who do high-impact work that's invisible (infrastructure, quality, coordination)

Knowing this: if you're in one of these groups, you need to be more intentional about making your impact visible. Not self-promoting aggressively, but ensuring your work is narrated to the people who need to see it.

## On Credit and Self-Advocacy

"Credit is infinitely divisible, and the atomic unit of success is a team, not an individual."

Senior PMs build career capital by making the people around them more successful, not by hoarding credit. This is both the ethical and the strategically correct approach: leaders who share credit become the kind of people others want to work with and advocate for.

Simultaneously: you must self-advocate. These are not in conflict. Give credit generously and make your own contributions legible to the people who need to see them.

## On Long-Term Career Planning

Rather than focusing on the next promotion six months away, ask: "What do I want the future to look like in five years?"

Then work backward: what skills do I need? What scope do I need to have operated at? What decisions do I need to have made? What relationships do I need?

Day-to-day work, when oriented against this five-year question, changes in texture. Decisions about which projects to take, which relationships to build, which skills to develop — all become clearer when you have a directional answer to the five-year question.

## On Learning While Busy

For learning by doing (the most effective mode for experienced PMs):
- Conduct product takedowns of your own and competitors' products — analyze decisions, hypothesize why they were made, evaluate whether they worked
- Actively engage in user feedback channels rather than receiving summaries
- Send thoughtful notes to product creators with observations (this also builds relationships)

Key insight: "The best PMs aren't the ones who are always looking inward. They look outward."

Internal-focused PMs improve incrementally. External-focused PMs learn from the entire market's experiments, not just their own.

## On Coaching Circles and Mentorship

Coaching circles are peer groups where people with similar career goals gather for conversations. They provide:
- Real-time peer feedback on decisions you're making
- Pattern recognition across organizations (what's an industry norm vs. a specific company's dysfunction)
- A reference class of people at similar stages

Building these peer relationships during periods when you don't need them is critical. "Invest in relationships during good times, not just when seeking help" — by the time you're looking for a new role or navigating a difficult situation, having these relationships already built is the difference between a fast resolution and a slow one.
