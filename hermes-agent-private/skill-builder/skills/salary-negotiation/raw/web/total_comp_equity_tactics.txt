## Understanding Total Compensation

Tech compensation is multi-dimensional. Most students think "salary" but the full package has 6+ components:

1. **Base salary** — regular paycheck, annual, taxed as ordinary income
2. **Equity (RSUs or options)** — often 30–60% of total comp at large tech companies
3. **Annual bonus** — target %, e.g., "15% target bonus" paid based on company + individual performance
4. **Signing bonus** — one-time payment, typically $10,000–$100,000, most negotiable
5. **Benefits** — health insurance, 401k match, etc. (non-cash, tax-advantaged, real value)
6. **Other perks** — relocation, commuter, phone stipend, equipment, professional development

The most common mistake: comparing offers by base salary only, ignoring equity, bonus, and signing bonus. Two offers at the same base salary can differ by $100,000+ in total annual compensation.

## Equity Valuation by Company Stage

Equity value is not what the recruiter says it is — it depends heavily on company stage and liquidity.

**Public companies (Google, Microsoft, Amazon, Meta)**
- RSUs have real, immediate value once vested
- Value at 80–90% of quoted amount (market risk, diversification cost)
- Vesting: typically 4-year, 1-year cliff, quarterly or monthly thereafter
- These are as real as cash once vested

**Pre-IPO growth companies (well-funded Series C–D)**
- Value at 60–90% depending on company trajectory and your risk tolerance
- Liquidity may be 2–5+ years away; company may never IPO
- Ask: "What is the most recent 409A valuation?" and "What is the preferred share liquidation preference?"

**Early-stage startups (Seed, Series A)**
- Treat equity as a lottery ticket; 80–90% of startups fail
- Base all financial planning on cash salary only; ignore equity claims
- Reject recruiter valuations based on hoped-for future valuations

**Key equity questions to always ask:**
- "What is the vesting schedule?" (confirm 1-year cliff, quarterly after)
- "How long after leaving do I have to exercise options?" (standard: 90 days; best: 5–10 years)
- "What is the strike price?" (options only)
- "What percentage of the company does this represent on a fully diluted basis?"
- "What is the current 409A valuation?"

## Research Tools for Compensation Data

Use the right tools. Generic salary sites are inaccurate for tech:

- **Avoid**: Glassdoor, PayScale, Comparably — wildly inaccurate for tech, mishandle equity
- **Use**: levels.fyi (total comp data by level, company, role)
- **Use**: H-1B visa filings (authoritative base salary data by company, role, location — public record)
- **Use**: Blind app (peer compensation data for large tech companies)
- **Use**: LinkedIn Salary (approximate but useful for ranges)
- **Use**: third-party technical recruiters (have real, current market data)

Strategy: before any negotiation, know the 25th, 50th, and 75th percentile total comp for your level and role at the target company. Levels.fyi often has this.

## The Competing Offers Playbook

The most reliable way to increase any offer is a competing offer from a company you'd actually work for. Never fabricate.

**How to use a competing offer:**
- Mention it directly to the recruiter: "I've received an offer from [Company X] at [total comp]. [Company Y] is my first choice, but I need to be thoughtful about the financial decision."
- The recruiter will almost always escalate to see what they can do
- Never reveal the exact number of the competing offer until you need to ("I'm looking for something in the [range] range")

**Timing multiple offers:**
- Start processes with large companies first (slower: Google takes 3–6 weeks, Amazon 2–4 weeks)
- Layer in startups later (faster: 1–2 week close timelines)
- When an offer comes in before others are ready, contact remaining companies: "I've received an offer with a deadline. I'm very interested in [Your Company] — can you accelerate?"

**Urgency script to accelerate a lagging company:**
"Hello [Recruiter], I wanted to update you — I've received a strong offer from [Company]. [Your Company] is my first choice. Since my timeline is compressed, is there any way to speed up the remaining steps?"

## The Negotiation Script: How to Actually Counter

**First call after receiving an offer:**
1. Thank them and express genuine excitement
2. Ask clarifying questions (level, vesting schedule, bonus target, equity cliff)
3. Say: "This is exciting. I need a few days to think it over carefully — can we reconnect on [day]?"
4. Do NOT say "yes," "no," or name a number on this call

**The counter call:**
Lead with enthusiasm, then make the ask around total comp:
- "I'm really excited about this opportunity and the team. In terms of total compensation, I was hoping to land closer to [number]. Is there any flexibility there?"
- Or, framed around competing offer: "I've received another offer at [company] for [range]. [Your Company] is my first choice — is there anything we can do on comp to make it easier for me to choose you?"

**If they can't move base:**
- "If the base is constrained, could we look at the equity or signing bonus?"
- "Would it be possible to increase the signing to [X]? That would help bridge the gap."

**Getting a number without anchoring low:**
If forced to give a range, anchor around an objective benchmark: "Based on levels.fyi data for this level at similar companies, total comp typically lands around [range]. I'd be looking for something in that area."

**The final ask (only when ready to sign):**
"If you can get to [X total comp], I'll sign today." This is a commitment, not a request. Use it only once, only when true.

## Negotiation Dimensions in Priority Order

When salary is capped, move through these in order (each is easier to approve than base salary):

1. **Equity refresh** — additional RSU grants, often approvable outside normal comp bands
2. **Signing bonus** — one-time, doesn't affect recurring payroll budgets, easiest to approve
3. **Vacation days** — low cost to employer, high value to you
4. **Remote flexibility** — saves you commute time; no monetary cost to employer
5. **Accelerated vesting cliff** — reducing from 1 year to 6 months; sometimes approvable
6. **Professional development budget** — conferences, courses, certifications
7. **Equipment** — laptop, desk, monitor stipend

## Key Principles from Candor's Research

- People who negotiate get an average 18.83% more than those who accept the first offer
- Average salary increase from negotiation: +$5,000 at minimum
- The best negotiators combine competitive (firm about outcome) and collaborative (warm in approach) styles
- Never negotiate via text/email alone — calls build relationship and allow the recruiter to "fight for you" internally
- Set a specific total comp target; be firm and specific about the number, vague about components
- Only negotiate if you mean it: genuine willingness to walk away if the number isn't met

## Common Mistakes in Offer Evaluation

1. **Comparing base salaries across offers** — ignoring equity and bonus can produce completely wrong ranking
2. **Accepting startup equity at face value** — treat early-stage equity as $0 for budgeting purposes
3. **Not asking what level the offer is** — a low end of a senior band beats a high end of a mid band for career progression
4. **Ignoring vesting schedule details** — a 4-year cliff vs. quarterly vesting is a massive practical difference
5. **Not asking the exercise window for options** — 90-day window vs. 10-year window is the difference between being able to afford to leave and being trapped
6. **Treating signing bonus as part of ongoing comp** — it's one-time; model annual comp without it
7. **Reneging on accepted offers casually** — while legal consequences are minimal, reputation damage is real in small industries
