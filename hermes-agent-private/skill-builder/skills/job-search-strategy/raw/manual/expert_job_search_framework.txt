Topic: Expert decision frameworks for tech job search — applicable to international students and domestic candidates
Country scope: Universal

## The Expert vs. Amateur Mental Model of Job Search

The biggest gap between successful and unsuccessful job seekers is not effort — it's mental model.

**Amateur model**: Send many applications → wait → get lucky.
**Expert model**: Build relationships in target companies → create pull → convert to interviews.

Amateur job seekers optimize the resume. Expert job seekers optimize the network. Amateur job seekers treat rejection as failure. Expert job seekers treat rejection as a filtering mechanism and maintain pipeline volume.

---

## The Job Search Funnel

Every job offer starts at the top of a funnel:
1. **Company identification** (which companies, in which industries, for which roles)
2. **Network activation** (who do I know at or near each target)
3. **Outreach** (cold email, warm intro, referral request)
4. **Conversation** (informational interview, recruiter screen)
5. **Application** (now the resume matters — it's validating, not discovering)
6. **Interview pipeline** (technical, behavioral, system design)
7. **Offer and negotiation**

Most students start at step 5. Most offers are won at steps 2–4.

## The Three Types of Job Search Activity (and how to allocate time)

**Type A — High leverage, uncomfortable**: Cold outreach, referral requests, networking with strangers. Response rate is low per message but highest per hour invested.
**Type B — Medium leverage, comfortable**: Applying to posted jobs, attending career fairs, submitting LinkedIn applications. Feels productive; converts at 4–10%.
**Type C — Low leverage, easy**: Browsing job boards, optimizing resume in the abstract, researching companies without acting. Feels busy but generates no pipeline.

Expert job seekers spend 50%+ of their time on Type A. Students default to Type C because it's comfortable.

## The Planning Horizon Problem

Most students start job searching too late. By the time you feel urgency, you've foreclosed your best options.

**What must happen before job search begins**:
- Target company list (10–30 companies with rationale)
- LinkedIn network activation (connect with everyone you know)
- Portfolio and proof of work completed and published
- Research into each target company's culture, team, tech stack

**How far in advance to start**:
- Internships: 9–12 months before start date
- Full-time entry: 6–12 months before graduation
- For international students with OPT deadlines: 12–18 months before graduation

The failure pattern: treating job search as a post-graduation problem, not a concurrent multi-semester project.

## The Referral Leverage Principle

A referral does not guarantee a job. It guarantees a human review of your resume. In a world where ATS systems filter out 75–90% of applicants before human eyes see them, this is worth enormous effort.

**Referral priority order**:
1. Close friend who works there and can vouch for you personally
2. Acquaintance who works there and knows your reputation
3. Mutual connection who will forward your info
4. Cold contact at the company with common ground
5. Cold contact at the company with no obvious connection

The further down this list you are, the more proof of worth you need in your outreach.

## What Actually Distinguishes Candidates

Hiring managers at tech companies consistently report that the candidates they hire have:

1. **Demonstrated outcomes, not just experience** — "Reduced latency by 40%" vs. "worked on backend systems"
2. **Curiosity about the specific company** — references to their actual products, tech stack, or recent news
3. **Clear communication under pressure** — not intelligence, but the ability to think out loud coherently
4. **Signal of long-term fit** — reasons why this company specifically, not just any company

The candidates who fail to get offers typically have:
- Strong credentials but poor ability to explain their work
- Generic interest (applied everywhere, prepared for nowhere)
- No narrative connecting their background to the role
- Inability to answer "tell me about yourself" in under 90 seconds

## Diagnostic Questions for Self-Assessment Before Applying

1. Can I explain my most impactful project in 90 seconds in a way that would make a non-technical hiring manager want to know more?
2. Do I have a specific reason why I want to work at each company I'm applying to — or am I just casting wide?
3. Have I spoken to at least one current employee at each target company in the last 6 months?
4. Is my LinkedIn profile compelling enough that a recruiter would reach out to me if they found me by search?
5. Have I practiced behavioral questions using STAR out loud with another person in the last 2 weeks?
6. Do I know the exact timeline of my work authorization status and have I communicated it clearly to recruiters?

## The Informational Interview: Most Underused Tool

An informational interview is a 20–30 minute conversation with someone at a company you're targeting. You are not asking for a job. You are asking for knowledge.

Why it works:
- Gets you to a human decision-maker before formal application
- Gives you inside knowledge (team culture, what problems they're solving, what they really want)
- Creates a social obligation: people are more likely to move your resume when they've met you
- May reveal unlisted positions

What to ask:
- "How did you end up in this role?"
- "What does strong performance look like in your first year?"
- "What's the biggest challenge your team is working through?"
- "Is there anything in my background I should be thinking about before applying?"

What not to do:
- Ask for a job or referral directly in the first conversation
- Show up unprepared (research their background before the call)
- Let it become one-directional (show genuine curiosity about their experience)

## The Compounding Effect of Early Networking

Networking done 12+ months before you need a job is categorically different from networking done at graduation.

Early networking:
- No pressure → more authentic conversations
- Time to build real relationships
- Time to demonstrate value back (share relevant articles, make introductions)
- Time to discover role types and companies you didn't know about

Late networking (desperation phase):
- High pressure → obvious to recipients
- Asking for immediate favors from strangers
- No time to build reciprocal relationships
- Limits you to only what's available right now

The expert principle: start building the network before you need it. The network you have at graduation is the one you built over the previous 2 years.

## Hidden Job Market: The Expert's Entry Points

The hidden job market (positions filled before posting) is most accessible through:

1. **Staying in touch with former managers and colleagues**: Most people land jobs through someone they already worked with.
2. **Being publicly visible**: Blog posts, talks, open source, LinkedIn articles — people who are discoverable get found.
3. **Following up with weak ties**: The acquaintance you met once at a conference often has more new information than your close friend in the same industry.
4. **Targeting companies in active growth**: Companies adding headcount frequently have unlisted roles that get filled before posting. Track funding announcements and hiring announcements.

## Heuristics from Experienced Job Search Coaches

1. **"Your resume is not your job search strategy — it's your job search support material."** The resume validates; the network finds the opportunity.
2. **"Apply to companies you've researched, not companies you've heard of."** Most students apply to brand names and ignore excellent companies they've never heard of.
3. **"Every rejection teaches you something. Only students who analyze rejections improve."** Ask for feedback. Track patterns. Adjust.
4. **"The people who get the most referrals give the most referrals."** Build a reputation as a connector, not just a job seeker.
5. **"LinkedIn is a search engine. Optimize for the recruiter who is looking, not the hiring manager who will read it."** Headlines, skills, and keywords drive inbound. Narrative drives conversion.
6. **"Practicing interviews is not studying for an interview — it is building a completely different skill."** Reading about STAR is not the same as drilling it out loud. Practice out loud, not in your head.
