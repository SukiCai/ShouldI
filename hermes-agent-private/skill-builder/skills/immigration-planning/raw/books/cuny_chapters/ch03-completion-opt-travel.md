# Chapter 3: Completion of Studies, Post-OPT, and Travel

## Core Idea
The 60-day grace period after graduation is a countdown, not a safety net. Every decision made in this window — apply for OPT, transfer, change status, or depart — determines whether the student can remain in the US legally. Missing the window means immediate unlawful presence with serious long-term immigration consequences.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **F-1 Grace Period: 60 Days**
  - Begins on program completion date (not graduation ceremony)
  - What you can do: apply for OPT, transfer to another SEVIS school, apply for change of status, prepare to depart
  - What you CANNOT do: work (unless EAD received), stay indefinitely
  - J-1 equivalent: 30 days (half the F-1 window)

- **Post-Completion OPT Process**
  - Apply 60–90 days BEFORE completion date OR within 60 days AFTER
  - DSO authorizes in SEVIS first; student then mails I-765 + fee to USCIS
  - Employment must begin within 90 days of OPT start date on EAD
  - 12 months available per degree level (associate, bachelor's, master's, doctoral — each independently eligible)

- **STEM OPT Extension**
  - 17-month extension (document says 17 months; current law is 24 months — use current USCIS rules)
  - Requirements: STEM degree from accredited US institution, currently in post-completion OPT, E-Verify employer, valid I-983 Training Plan
  - Must apply before current OPT expires

- **OPT Unemployment Limits**
  - 90 days maximum unemployment on standard post-completion OPT (cumulative)
  - 150 days cumulative during STEM extension period
  - Exceeding limits = automatic OPT termination and out-of-status

- **Transfer While on OPT**
  - OPT is school-specific; authorization terminates automatically when transfer release date is set
  - OPT cannot be transferred to the new school
  - Strategy: if planning to continue OPT, set transfer release date AFTER OPT end date

- **Travel and Re-Entry During OPT**
  Required documents for CBP re-entry during OPT:
  1. Valid passport + unexpired F-1 visa stamp
  2. I-20 endorsed for travel with "OPT Authorization" notation
  3. EAD card from USCIS
  4. Employment letter from OPT employer confirming employment (and salary if renewing visa abroad)

- **Program/Status Termination Triggers for F-1**
  DSO must terminate SEVIS record if:
  - Student fails to register within 30 days of registration deadline after entry
  - Failed to maintain full-time course load
  - Failed to make normal academic progress
  - Failed to report address change within 15 days
  - Engaged in unauthorized work
  - Voluntarily withdrew from program

## Key Concepts
- **Program Completion Date**: The date the student fulfills all academic requirements — often differs from graduation ceremony date; this is what starts the 60-day clock
- **Grace Period Unemployment**: Unemployment days on OPT accumulate during the grace period countdown; students must track days from OPT start date, not completion date
- **I-20 Travel Endorsement**: DSO must sign the I-20 for travel within the past 6 months; an unsigned or expired-travel-signature I-20 will result in re-entry denial
- **Multiple Degree OPT**: Each degree level (bachelor's, master's, PhD) independently qualifies for its own 12-month OPT; strategic enrollment in a new degree resets eligibility

## Mental Models
- The 60-day grace period is a transition window, not a vacation. Every action must happen within it or the student falls out of status by default.
- Treat unemployment days like a bank balance: you start with 90, every day without a job (or EAD pending) spends one. Know your balance at all times.
- OPT is not portable: it belongs to the school that authorized it. Transferring schools during OPT = losing OPT.

## Anti-patterns
- **Waiting until after graduation to start OPT application**: Processing takes 3–5 months. Students who apply on graduation day may have a multi-month work authorization gap.
- **Traveling abroad during OPT without updated I-20**: Common cause of re-entry denial. DSO must endorse I-20 travel signature before departure.
- **Not tracking unemployment days**: 90 days sounds like a lot; it disappears quickly between jobs and during USCIS processing delays. One day over = status terminated.
- **Assuming OPT transfers with school**: OPT is explicitly school-specific; zero exceptions.
- **Confusing completion date with ceremony date**: Graduation ceremonies are often months after the actual academic completion. The 60-day clock starts at academic completion.

## Worked Example
Student Priya (F-1, CS Master's) finishes coursework December 15. Graduation ceremony is May. She assumes the 60-day grace period starts in May. **Wrong**: the 60 days started December 15. By late February the grace period expired — Priya is now unlawfully present. **Correct**: apply for OPT immediately in October (90 days before December completion); EAD arrives by January; begin work on authorized start date.

## Key Takeaways
1. Grace period starts at academic completion, not graduation ceremony — these are often months apart
2. Apply for OPT 60–90 days BEFORE graduation, not after
3. Track unemployment days like a depleting resource; 90 days is the hard limit
4. I-20 must be travel-endorsed before any international travel during OPT
5. OPT is school-specific and terminates automatically when transfer to another school is released
