# Chapter 4: Potential Risks, Violations, and Failure Patterns

## Core Idea
F-1 and J-1 status violations are binary: you are either in status or you are not. There is no partial compliance. The most dangerous aspect is that many violations are invisible to the student at the time they occur — the student only discovers they are out of status months later when applying for OPT, H-1B, or re-entry. By then, the violation has been accumulating unlawful presence.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **Institutional Risk Framework (University Perspective)**
  - DHS can revoke F-1 certification = university cannot admit any more F-1 students
  - State Dept can revoke J-1 Exchange Visitor Program designation = no more J-1 students university-wide
  - CUNY's entire SEVIS program depends on each campus DSO doing their job correctly

- **Individual Student Violation Categories**
  F-1 status termination triggers:
  1. Failed to register within 30 days of registration deadline after entry
  2. Approved change of status (voluntary, terminates F-1)
  3. Failed to maintain full-time course load
  4. Failed to make normal academic progress toward degree
  5. Failed to report address change within 15 days of moving
  6. Engaged in unauthorized employment (even one day)

  J-1 status termination triggers (more severe — students must leave IMMEDIATELY):
  1. Criminal conviction
  2. Institutional disciplinary action
  3. Unauthorized employment
  4. Failure to pursue Exchange Visitor program activities
  5. Failure to report address change within 10 days
  6. Failure to maintain full-time enrollment
  7. Failure to maintain health insurance (willful failure = termination, cannot be reinstated)
  8. Violation of sponsor (university) rules

- **Improper Financial Documentation Risk**
  - Universities that issue I-20s without verifying adequate financial sponsorship risk losing SEVIS certification
  - For students: insufficient funds during studies = potential status issues if financial hardship leads to unauthorized work

- **Health Insurance — J-1 Mandate vs. F-1 Advisory**
  - J-1: Health insurance is federally MANDATED; willful failure = status termination, and J-1 status CANNOT be reinstated after health insurance violation
  - F-1: Health insurance strongly recommended but not federally required; however, lack of it is a financial risk that can lead to status-threatening decisions

## Key Concepts
- **Willful Violation**: Knowing you are violating a rule and doing it anyway; disqualifies from reinstatement
- **Unlawful Presence**: Begins accumulating immediately upon status violation; 180+ days = 3-year bar to re-entry; 365+ days = 10-year bar
- **Reinstatement**: USCIS application to restore F-1 status; available only if: out of status <5 months, no willful violations, no criminal record
- **J-1 Two-Year Home Residency Requirement**: Some J-1 students must return to home country for 2 years before applying for H or L visas or immigrant status; not applicable to all J-1 holders

## Mental Models
- Think of F-1 status as a glass wall: once broken, the pieces don't go back together without a formal USCIS reinstatement process
- J-1 violations are more severe than F-1: J-1 termination means immediate departure required; no grace period for the violation itself
- The 5-month reinstatement window for F-1 is a hard deadline: every day past 5 months significantly reduces reinstatement chances
- Address change reporting is often overlooked: 15-day rule for F-1, 10-day rule for J-1; failure accumulates as a reportable violation in SEVIS

## Anti-patterns
- **Assuming graduation ends all reporting obligations**: Status violations that occurred before graduation can surface during OPT or H-1B processing, causing denial
- **Working one day before EAD arrives**: Students rationalize "the EAD is almost here." One unauthorized work day = permanent record in SEVIS
- **Changing apartments without notifying DSO within 15 days**: Students consistently underestimate this requirement; it is a status violation
- **J-1 student letting health insurance lapse**: A willful failure; cannot be reinstated. Students who are healthy assume they don't need it and face permanent status termination
- **Disclosing status violation on H-1B forms long after the fact**: Immigration history is permanent; discrepancies between what students report and what SEVIS records show are identified during H-1B and green card processing

## Worked Example
Student Carlos (F-1) moves apartments in September, forgets to update his address with DSO. In March, his H-1B petition is filed. USCIS cross-references SEVIS and finds a reportable event (address change) was never reported. The H-1B petition gets an RFE (Request for Evidence) asking about compliance history. Carlos's lawyer must now explain and document the address update. Delay: 3–4 months. This started with failing to send one email.

## Key Takeaways
1. Unauthorized employment — even one day — is the most severe individual violation; it ends F-1 status with no reinstatement option in most cases
2. J-1 health insurance failure is uniquely dangerous: willful failure = status termination AND no reinstatement path
3. Address changes must be reported within 15 days (F-1) or 10 days (J-1) — this is one of the most overlooked yet consistently enforced requirements
4. Unlawful presence accumulates from the date of violation, not the date of discovery; 180+ days has serious multi-year re-entry consequences
5. Reinstatement requires: <5 months out of status + no willful violations + no criminal record + currently pursuing/planning to pursue full-time study
