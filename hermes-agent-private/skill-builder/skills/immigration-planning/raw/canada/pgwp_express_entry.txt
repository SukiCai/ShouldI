## The Canada Immigration Funnel for International Students

Canada's pathway is more structured than the US — it's a points-based system rather than a lottery. The basic funnel:
  Study in Canada → PGWP (work permit after graduation) → Build Canadian experience → Express Entry (Canadian Experience Class) → Permanent Residence

The key advantage over the US: no lottery. If you accumulate enough CRS points, you will eventually receive an Invitation to Apply (ITA). The challenge is that cut-off scores have risen and the system has become more competitive.

## PGWP — What Changed in 2024–2025

### Language Requirement (effective November 1, 2024)
All new PGWP applications now require proof of English or French language proficiency:
- University degrees (Bachelor's, Master's, PhD): CLB 7 (IELTS Academic 6.0 per band)
- College non-degree programs (diplomas, certificates): CLB 5 (IELTS Academic 5.0 per band)
- This applies even if you studied in English — language test required

### Program Eligibility Overhaul (effective June 25, 2025)
For students who applied for their study permit on or after June 25, 2025:
- 119 new fields added (healthcare, social services, education, skilled trades)
- 178 fields removed (most transport programs, many agriculture programs, some traditional trades)
- University degrees (bachelor's and above) in any field remain eligible — changes primarily affect diplomas and certificates

**Who is protected:** Students who applied before June 25, 2025 retain previous rules regardless of program changes.

**Strategic implication:** Program selection now directly affects PGWP eligibility for diploma/certificate students. This is a major hidden tradeoff — a cheaper diploma program may not generate a PGWP.

### PGWP Duration
- Study programs less than 8 months: no PGWP
- Study programs 8 months to less than 2 years: PGWP = length of study program
- Study programs 2 years or more: PGWP = 3 years (maximum)

## Express Entry — How the Points System Works

Express Entry manages three federal immigration programs: Federal Skilled Worker (FSW), Canadian Experience Class (CEC), and Federal Skilled Trades (FST).

International students with Canadian work experience almost exclusively use CEC.

### CRS Score Components (what matters most)
1. Language proficiency (English and French — bilingualism is a significant multiplier)
2. Canadian work experience (CEC requires minimum 1 year of skilled work in Canada in NOC TEER 0, 1, 2, or 3)
3. Education (Canadian or foreign credentials, ECA required for foreign education)
4. Age (peak points at 20–29, decreasing after 30)
5. Spousal factors (spouse's language + education add points)

### Current CRS Cut-Offs (as of 2025)
- General Express Entry draws: ~520+ CRS
- Canadian Experience Class (CEC)-specific draws: typically 531–534 in late 2025
- French language draws: 379–428 CRS (dramatically lower cut-off)
- PNP-enhanced draws: CRS + 600 provincial nomination points = almost guaranteed ITA

### The Single Most Important CRS Lever: French
French proficiency at CLB 7 or higher adds significant CRS points AND makes candidates eligible for the lowest cut-off draws. For non-French speakers, investing 6–12 months in French language training often yields higher return on CRS points than almost any other action.

### Job Offer Points: Removed
As of March 25, 2025, job offer points were removed from the CRS scoring system. A job offer is still relevant for some program eligibility criteria, but no longer boosts your CRS score.

## Provincial Nominee Programs (PNP) — The 600-Point Shortcut

A provincial nomination adds 600 points to your CRS score — effectively guaranteeing an ITA regardless of your base CRS. Each province has its own nomination criteria:

- **Ontario Immigrant Nominee Program (OINP)**: Draws from the Express Entry pool, often targeting specific NOC codes
- **British Columbia PNP (BC PNP)**: Skills Immigration stream, draws held weekly, NOC-specific
- **Alberta Advantage Immigration Program**: Draws from Express Entry, frequently higher CRS minimums
- **Saskatchewan, Manitoba, Nova Scotia**: Often lower base score requirements, may require job offer or connections to the province

Strategy: Monitor PNP draws for your occupation code. A provincial nomination is often faster than waiting for a federal draw.

## What Most International Students Get Wrong About Canadian Immigration

**Mistake 1: Treating PGWP as guaranteed**
Many students enroll in programs without verifying PGWP eligibility. Diploma and certificate students especially: if your program isn't on the eligible list and you applied after June 25, 2025, you have no PGWP.

**Mistake 2: Ignoring CRS score from day one**
Students who don't track their CRS score until they're in the PGWP period are often surprised. CRS calculators are publicly available — run your numbers before you even start working and identify gaps early (language, education, age penalty).

**Mistake 3: Underestimating the French language advantage**
Most Mandarin/Hindi/Korean-speaking students dismiss French. But it's the single highest-ROI CRS lever available. Even B1-level French with a clear path to B2/CLB 7 significantly changes immigration trajectory.

**Mistake 4: Not understanding NOC TEER categories**
CEC requires 1 year of skilled work in NOC TEER 0, 1, 2, or 3. Many students work in retail, food service, or manual labor during and after school (NOC TEER 4 or 5) and discover this doesn't count toward CEC eligibility.

**Mistake 5: Waiting for PR before planning**
By the time you're in the PGWP period, most of your CRS-relevant decisions (program choice, language scores, education credentials) are already locked. Planning should happen before you choose which program to enroll in.

**Mistake 6: Overlooking spousal CRS boost**
If you're married or in a common-law partnership, your spouse's education and language scores add meaningful CRS points. Getting your spouse to take IELTS can be worth 10–40 extra CRS points.

## Key Diagnostic Questions for Canada Immigration Path

1. What is your program type (university degree vs. college diploma/certificate)? (determines PGWP eligibility risk)
2. When did you apply for your study permit? (determines which PGWP rules apply to you)
3. What is your current CRS score? (run the federal calculator now if you haven't)
4. What is your French language proficiency? (single highest-ROI improvement lever)
5. What is your NOC TEER code for the work you plan to do? (determines CEC eligibility)
6. Does your province have an active PNP stream for your occupation? (600-point shortcut)
7. Is your education credential recognized in Canada? (foreign credentials require ECA from designated organizations)
8. What is your age now and at graduation? (CRS age penalty starts after 29, accelerates after 35)
