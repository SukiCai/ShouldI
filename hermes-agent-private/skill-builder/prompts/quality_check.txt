## Evaluation Rubric

Score each dimension 1–5:
- 5: Excellent — genuinely expert, specific, non-obvious
- 4: Good — mostly strong with minor gaps
- 3: Adequate — useful but too generic in places
- 2: Weak — mostly generic advice that any informed person would give
- 1: Poor — wrong, vague, or actively misleading

### Dimensions

**1. Expert Framing (the opening section)**
Does it capture how an expert *thinks*, not just what they know? Is it counterintuitive in at least one meaningful way? Does it name the specific wrong framings that users bring — and correct them? A generic "this is a complex topic" is not expert framing.

**2. Heuristics Specificity**
Are heuristics specific and actionable with clear conditions? ("Run the CRS calculator before choosing a program, not after graduation" = specific. "Plan ahead" = worthless.) Do they include the reasoning (why)? Are they non-obvious — things a novice wouldn't already know?

**3. Diagnostic Questions Quality**
Do the diagnostic questions reveal something a novice wouldn't think to ask? Do they differentiate expert vs. naive thinking? Can they actually be used in a real conversation? "What are your goals?" is not a diagnostic question.

**4. Failure Patterns Accuracy**
Are the failure patterns things that actually happen in the real world — not just logical possibilities? Do they include recognizable warning signs? Are they specific enough that a user could identify "that's me"?

**5. Hidden Tradeoffs Depth**
Are the tradeoffs genuinely non-obvious? Or are they just "pros and cons"? A good hidden tradeoff is one where the downside is invisible at the moment of decision and only becomes clear later.

**6. Industry Realities Calibration**
Is the "belief" in each belief/reality pair something people actually believe (not a straw man)? Is the "reality" genuinely surprising and different from the belief? Are the facts accurate and current?

**7. Junior vs. Senior Thinking Table**
Are the dimensions meaningful differentiators — things that actually separate novice from expert thinking? Is the "senior" pattern genuinely better, or just more experienced-sounding? Would this table help an AI give noticeably better advice?

**8. Overall Actionability**
Could an AI assistant using this skill give meaningfully better advice than without it? Is there enough structure for the AI to diagnose situations, not just recite information? Is anything missing that would significantly improve the skill?
