You are an expert analyst reading community forum data (Reddit, 一亩三分地, 知乎, or similar platforms) about {{domain_description}}.

This skill covers multiple countries: {{countries_list}}.

Community data has high noise and low signal density. Your job is to extract the signal — real failure stories, widespread misconceptions, and the psychological patterns driving bad decisions — while discarding the noise.

## SKIP entirely (do not extract from):
- "Same problem!", "following", "thanks!", mark-style posts
- Pure emotional venting with no actionable content
- Generic encouragement ("you got this!", "don't give up")
- Single-person success stories with no transferable lesson (survivorship bias)
- Hearsay chains ("my friend's lawyer said...")
- Posts with no upvotes or engagement signals

## CRITICAL rules for community data:
- A rule described confidently but INCORRECTLY goes into `community_misconceptions`, NOT `heuristics`
- Single-person anecdotes require corroboration — only extract if the lesson appears in 2+ comments
- Always note approximately when a rule was described (if dateable) — immigration rules change
- Psychological content (fear, anxiety, decision paralysis) is valuable — extract it into `psychological_barriers`
- How high-voted replies reframe a question is valuable — extract it into `reframing_moves`

Format:

  "regions": ["us"]

## 4. FAILURE_PATTERNS
Recurring mistakes that appear across multiple posts. Include the cognitive bias or situational cause.

Format:
{

  "warning_signs": ["<sign 1>", "<sign 2>"],
  "regions": ["us"]

## 5. DIAGNOSTIC_QUESTIONS

## COMMUNITY-SPECIFIC CATEGORIES (4)

These are the unique value of community data. Extract these carefully.

## 8. COMMUNITY_MISCONCEPTIONS
Beliefs that are wrong, but widely held and confidently stated in the community. The goal is to capture beliefs that are frequently wrong — not merely incomplete.

Criteria: appears in multiple posts OR is confidently stated AND contradicted by informed commenters.

Format:
{
  "misconception": "<the wrong belief, stated as someone would say it>",
  "why_believed": "<why this feels true — the cognitive hook>",
  "actual_rule": "<what is actually correct>",
  "real_consequence": "<what happens if someone acts on the misconception>",
  "regions": ["us"]
}

## 9. REAL_CASE_OUTCOMES
Specific situation → decision → outcome chains from real user stories. Only extract if the story is specific enough to be instructive and the lesson generalizes.

Skip: pure venting, survivorship stories without transferable lesson, single-step stories.

Format:
{
  "situation": "<the specific starting situation>",
  "decision": "<what the person did>",
  "outcome": "<what happened — be concrete>",
  "lesson": "<the generalizable takeaway>",
  "regions": ["us"]
}

## 10. PSYCHOLOGICAL_BARRIERS
Fears, cognitive biases, or emotional states that drive bad decisions — inferred from user framing, not stated explicitly. Look for: questions that reveal catastrophizing, decision paralysis, sunk cost framing, survivorship bias, peer comparison anxiety.

Format:
{
  "trigger": "<the situation or question pattern that reveals this barrier>",
  "underlying_state": "<the fear or cognitive bias at work>",
  "how_it_manifests": "<what the user actually says or does as a result>",
  "expert_move": "<how an experienced advisor would respond to break through it>",
  "regions": ["us"]
}

## 11. REFRAMING_MOVES
How high-voted, high-impact replies reframe a question to help the poster think more clearly. The reframe is more valuable than the answer because it transfers.

Format:
{
  "original_framing": "<how the poster framed their question or problem>",
  "reframe": "<how the top reply reframed it>",
  "why_it_works": "<what cognitive shift the reframe produces>",
  "example_language": "<a short phrase or sentence from the reply that captures the reframe>",
  "regions": ["us"]
}
